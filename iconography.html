Reading the Painting

Figures, iconography, and inscriptions

Reading a Newari narrative painting begins with identification: recognizing each deity by their attributes, each royal figure by their dress, each ritual object and gesture by its convention. Alongside the figures runs the writing, a line of Newari script above each register, narrating the scenes below it. Both are read together here.

Methodology

Part A · Figures & Iconography

The study of the painting proceeds in three steps.

  1. Tracing. The painting is traced on tracing paper to separate the objects and characters from the background, which is often painted in the same color and has very faint borders. Tracing gives clear versions of each composition and keeps every working mark off the painting itself, so nothing added in study can be mistaken for the original.
  2. Iconographic study. Each deity, figure, and object is identified by its attributes, following the conventions of Newar painting.
  3. Relationship study. The painting and the inscriptions are read against each other, tying it all together to see whether everything makes sense.

Scene Map

How the painting is divided

The painting reads like a page. Three horizontal registers run the length of the cloth, each crowned by a line of golden inscription that narrates it. Each register divides into two sections, and each section into scenes, read left to right, top to bottom. Select any scene to open it in Story Mode.

Part B · Inscriptions

Each line, painted in golden yellow along a black band, was read directly from high resolution photography and carried through four stages, undertaken with Dr. Naresh Man Bajracharya and Dr. Puspa Ratna Sakya.

  1. Inscription reading. The lines are read directly from the cloth, character by character.
  2. Transliteration. The reading is carried from the Newari (Nepal Bhasa) script into Devanagari.
  3. Romanization. The Devanagari is mapped into the Latin alphabet using diacritical marks, so that non-Latin sounds keep their precision.
  4. Study of the script. The reading is then worked through by copying all of the script from each line into a clear grid. Much of the writing in the painting is damaged and illegible, so boxes are left empty for illegible characters and brackets are used for readings that are unsure but grounded in the professional intuition of Dr. Naresh and Dr. Puspa.

Each line of inscription is one continuous whole. The painting's scenes divide it visually, but the writing reads across those divisions, so the inscriptions are studied line by line, not scene by scene.

What the inscriptions name

The painting identifies its own cast. Working from the transliterated inscriptions, Dr. Naresh Man Bajracharya and Dr. Puspa Ratna Sakya indexed every named deity, king, person, object, place, action, and date across the three lines, then classified them so the painting's world can be taken in at a glance.

The Scripts Chart

Every legible character from every line was copied into gridded charts, one cell per character, following the conventions described in the methodology above. The chart sections were then joined back into a single continuous line each, reading left to right. Because portions of each line are damaged, the inscriptions are best read as connected passages rather than word-perfect sentences. A word-by-word rendering from Newari into Nepali was prepared for each line; the English translations, prepared with Dr. Bajracharya, are forthcoming.

How to read the transcriptions

A dash (–) marks one illegible character; characters inside parentheses ( ) are unclear readings that are inferred from the expert opinion of Dr. Naresh and Dr. Puspa; square brackets [ ] are readings beneath another character or space; ⟨i⟩ and ⟨?⟩ mark stray vowel signs and unidentifiable traces respectively; ॥✽॥ is a pair of daṇḍas enclosing a drawn auspicious symbol.

The painting dates itself

The third line closes with a date written in Nepal Sambat, the calendar of the Newar people: the year 832. Nepal Sambat began in 880 CE, so adding 880 to a Nepal Sambat year gives the year in the common era. Nepal Sambat 832 is 1712 CE, the very date the museum records for the painting. By the same count, the present year, 2026, is Nepal Sambat 1146.

The line in brief

A summary of what the fragment records will appear here.

A king the date agrees with

This line mentions the name of King Mahindra Malla. Looking at the rulers of Patan during the Malla dynasty, which ran from 1201 to 1769 CE, King Vira Mahindra Malla ruled Patan from 1709 to 1715 CE. The date read on the painting, 1712 CE, falls exactly during his ruling period. Vira is the romanized diacritical form of "Bir", which means heroic in Newari (Nepal Bhasa).

01

The Painting

A story told across two meters of cloth

Painted in Nepal in 1712, this bilampau, a horizontal narrative painting of the Newar tradition, tells the legend of Bunga Dyo, the Red Avalokiteshvara, a deity beloved in the Kathmandu Valley for more than a millennium. He is known by many names: Karunamaya, the Compassionate One; Rato Matsyendranath; the god of Bungamati. Across three registers, episode follows episode: gods and kings, processions and offerings, rendered in the deep vermilion and forest green of Newari painting.

A line of golden inscription runs above each register, written in an old form of Prachalit, the Newari script, narrating the scenes it crowns. Time has worn parts of the cloth and its writing, but through transliteration and translation the story it preserves can be read again.

This archive presents the painting in high resolution, every scene and every inscription, together with the research that makes them legible: a study undertaken with the guidance of scholars of Newar Buddhism in Nepal and art historians in the United States.

03

The Project

Why preserve this painting digitally?

These bilampaus were made to be read. For decades, paintings like this one have left the Kathmandu Valley for private collections and distant museums, admired for their beauty while their inscriptions went unread and their rituals and donors were forgotten. A painting that cannot be read is silenced: it becomes a decorative object rather than a historical document.

This project began when its author, a Nepali student from Kathmandu, found that he could study the great bilampaus of his own heritage only through low resolution photographs and near empty catalogue records. Its purpose is to give this painting its voice back: to read, translate, and share what is written on the cloth, and to set the living tradition beside it.

A personal introduction from the project's author will appear here.

04

The Living Tradition

Bunga Dyo and the chariot festival

Bunga Dyo is not a figure of the past. Revered by Buddhists and Hindus alike as a bringer of rain and compassion, the deity divides each year between the village of Bungamati, his original home and the source of his name, and a shrine in Patan.

Each year, in one of the oldest and grandest processions of the Kathmandu Valley, Bunga Dyo is drawn through the streets in a towering wooden chariot built anew for the journey. The festival commemorates the deity's arrival in the valley and the end of a legendary twelve year drought. The painting in this archive belongs to that living story: episodes of the legend, painted three centuries ago, of a tradition that continues today.

The Legend

The story the painting tells

The legend of Bunga Dya survives in more than one telling. Two are given here in full: the story as it is told in the valley today, and the story the painting itself follows.

What the locals say

Among the many jatras of the valley, the Rato Machhindranath Jatra of Patan is the longest and features the tallest chariot. The deity it honors is known by many names: Karunamaya, Aryavalokiteshvara, Lokeshwor, and, in Patan and Bungamati, Bunga Dya. This is one telling, drawn from the Newa chronicle, of how that deity came to the valley. It is a weave of myth, belief, and remembered history.

In the old days, King Gunakamadev ruled Nepal Mandal (present-day central Nepal). Through his line and a promise made to Lord Narayan, the valley was told it could have rice and never go hungry, but only when the lord of grain, Karunamaya, was present to fill the ripening husks. Karunamaya, it was foretold, would be born in Kamuni, the land of the Yakshas (spirits), as the youngest of five hundred sons. During King Narendradev's reign, a drought fell upon the valley. The yogi Gorakhnath, defeated in a contest and annoyed by it, seized all the Naags, the bringers of rain, and sat upon them in unbroken meditation at Mrigasthali. Season after season, no rain fell, and famine spread.

When no scholar could name the cause, the king turned to the learned guru Shantikar Vajracharya. Only one thing would raise Gorakhnath, Vajracharya said: the coming of his own guru, Karunamaya Lokeshwor, whom the yogi would rise to honor, freeing the Naags.

Lokeshwor was then living in Kamuni. King Narendradev, along with a tantric priest named Bandhudatta and a porter named Rathan Chakra, set out to bring Karunamaya. On the way, they won the help of Karkotaka, king of the Naags, who bridged a petrifying river with his own body. Upon reaching Kamuni, they gained entry to the Yaksha king's palace by posing as commoners, but the Yakshani queen discovered their plan and refused to give up her son. However, Bandhudatta performed a rite that drew Karunamaya out as a bee and caught him in a golden Kalash (vessel). With four Bhairavs to carry the deity and seeds sown along the road to guide his eventual return, they journeyed back toward the valley. Along the way, Bandhudatta bound the Yakshani queen to a tree because she was trying to take Karunamaya back.

When they reached Bunga, Gorakhnath sensed his guru and rose from his meditation. The Nags were freed, and the rain returned. It was agreed that Karunamaya would share two homes, returning to Bunga after each procession, and so he does to this day. A shrine was raised in Yala, and the image of Karunamaya was made with the Kalash at its heart, from the soil where the deity's past life lay buried.

A chariot festival was founded in his honor. The chariot rises thirty-two hands for the thirty-two auspicious signs; its four wheels are the four Bhairavs, Karkotaka its main beam, and the Nags its ropes. The festival begins on Baishakh Sukla Pratipada, the first day of the bright fortnight of Baisakh. The image is carried from its shrine to the waiting chariot at Pulchowk and drawn through Gabahal, Sundhara, and Lagankhel, where the chariot circles the tree remembered as the mother of the deity. It comes at last to Jawalakhel, where the sacred bhoto (vest) is shown to the gathered valley, and the long festival closes.

What the painting says

In the Tretayuga, during the time of Lord Vishwabhu, Shree Karunamaya, living in the Sukhavathi Bhuvan, saw the suffering beings in the country of Magadha and went to rescue them. Because there had been no rain for twenty years, the grass and trees had not grown properly, and there was nothing to eat or drink. This had driven the people to kill one another and eat each other’s flesh and blood to survive.

In this way, the people of Magadha fought among themselves. The strong killed the weak, and they committed great sins. Seeing this, Shree Karunamaya came to the land disguised as a man, influenced them with his holy ray, and asked them, “O men, why do you fight and kill each other, eat the flesh and blood of men, commit sins, and do evil deeds like the Bhutayakshas?”

Under the influence of Karunamaya’s holy ray, the wicked people of Magadha, who had lost sight of humanity, calmed down and paid attention to him. They gathered before Karunamaya and told him everything that had happened. “O great sage, our country of Magadha is facing great difficulties. It has not rained in this country for 20 years. Therefore, we are unable to bear the pain of not being able to eat or drink. Because of this, compassion and love are not the only things that flow from our hearts; jealousy and anger also arise. In this way, we have suffered for a long time. If you have the power to pacify this famine, then please protect us all and provide us with an abundance of food.”

After hearing the people of Magadha, the Bodhisattva of Compassion ascended to the sky and immediately caused clouds to gather and rain to fall. Everyone was surprised to see rain after 20 years, and they quickly drank the nectar-like water to quench their thirst. He, the Compassionate One, again brought many kinds of food and sweets. All the people in the country were amazed to see rice, wheat, and other food items also raining down, so everyone took as much as they could home. Again, Karunamaya rained many kinds of gold, silver, and other metal objects. All the residents of Magadha were surprised and happily took as much as they could, safely storing it in their coffers. They collected the items with laughter and joy.

At that time, everyone was in a good mood and gathered to discuss who had influenced the event, coming up with many arguments and theories. Then, Shree Karunamaya entered the body of an old man, went to the group of people who were gathering, and said, “O people of the country. You believe this is due to the grace of some god who has brought auspiciousness or prosperity to our country. However, no one living in heaven or hell can experience such prosperity. You should know that it is due to the grace of Shree Karunamaya Lokeshwor, the Lord of the universe. I will briefly explain his greatness. Listen carefully and with one mind.”

He continued, “Shree Karunamaya is the son of the Tathagata. He is the master of the elements of desire, form, and the inanimate, and is saving all sentient beings, showing the path of righteousness to those who do not see it. He is like an umbrella on a hot, sunny day, like a river to the thirsty, like a plant to the hungry, like a doctor to the sick, and the one who saves those in hell by granting them merit in Nirvana. Such a place is a place of salvation. If you remember the name of Karunamaya and observe the Bodhicharya fast, we will all be able to hear the Dharma story of Amitabha in the garden of happiness.” After saying this, the old man left.

From that time on, Magadha received rainfall periodically, and the canals filled with abundant water. Shree Karunamaya then left the old man’s body and went to save other beings. These things are mentioned in the Gunakaranda Vyuha.
